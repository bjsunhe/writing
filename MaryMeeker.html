On January 11, 2008, a day when the broader markets were only down three-quarters of 1%, American Express, led by legendary CEO Ken Chenault, dropped 10% when Oppenheimer analyst Meredith Whitney lowered her rating of the firm. That same day, Dreamworks dropped 3% when Jessica Reif-Cohen, an analyst at Merrill, lowered her rating of the company, led by entertainment mogul Jeffery Katzenberg. Companies spend millions of dollars to build a strong reputation because they believe that it is directly related to their overall market value. But a company's reputation is not the only one that people consider when making decisions sunch as whether to invest or not; there is also the CEO's reputation and the analyst's. We wanted to understand the ways in which each of these affect firms, and how the market behaved when confronted with multiple trusted sources of information. To explore which type of reputation has a greater impact, we drew on large databases of corporate, financial, and market information compiled over a 13-year period. We studied the effect of analyst, CEO, and firm reputation on the size of the market reactions to analyst upgrades and downgrades - in other words, whether the stock price went up or down after an analyst issued a recommendation about whether to buy, hold, or sell a firm's stock. We measured CEO reputation by using the number of leadership awards bestowed on a chief in the five years previous to a given year by seven leading business magazines. We measured analyst reputation the standard way - by seeing which analysts were selected to one of the all-American teams published annually by Institutional Investor magazine. And we scored firm reputation by using lists of the most admired firms. In all, we studied about 19,500 downgrades and 17,400 upgrades from 1996 to 2008. Our results were recently published in the Academy of Management Journal. With a massive literature devoted to leadership and American companies reportedly spending $14 billion a year to teach it, one might expect that a CEO's reputation would outshine those of even well-regarded analysts. Our study found otherwise. We found that a downgrade by a star analyst causes tremendous stock valuation changes that are not offset by the CEO having a good reputation. In other words, star analysts' reputations overwhelmed those of the CEOs they were covering in terms of shareholder reaction - even when star analysts downgraded firms run by star CEOs. Specifically, we found that a downgrade by star analysts increased the negative market reaction by 40%, regardless of a CEO's reputation. Thus, when a star analyst issues a downgrade, the CEO's reputation has almost no effect on the market reaction. In marked contrast, CEO reputation does buffer the stock market reaction to downgrades by regular analysts. The impact of a downgrade by non-star analysts was reduced by 3% when the CEO had a strong reputation. So CEO reputation influenced the market reaction to downgrades only when the analyst making the downgrade was not recognized as a star. We found a similar pattern for upgrades. Once again, star analysts rating a firm highly had the greatest impact on the market: their ratings increased the positive market reaction by 42%. What does this all mean? Our findings suggest that while reputation clearly matters, all reputations are not considered equally. When different reputations collide, some are going to matter more than others. Investors tended to weigh analysts' expertise more heavily than others' when making decisions. We believe this is because investors who are buying and selling stock view an analyst's reputation as most relevant to the immediate decision. CEO and firm reputation are based on factors that are less directly relevant to the specific decision to buy or sell at any given moment. This finding was somewhat surprising, however, given how many other studies have found large positive effects for CEO and firm reputation. This scenario of weighing multiple reputations appears in many other contexts, from education markets to consumer products. For instance, universities have broad reputations, but so do individual departments, and even individual professors. Similarly, consumers are often faced with making a purchasing decision when there are multiple reputations, such as that of the product itself and its brand, as well as the reputation of store selling the product. Consequently, it is important to understand which reputation is likely to matter most. If companies are going to focus time and resources on building and maintaining their reputations, it is important to understand when it is going to matter, and when it isn't. Firms often wonder how effectivre their brands are, or what their reputation is actually doing, and they often focus on multiple things at once. Our study suggests that people may not be balancing multiple sources of information, but may instead rely on the one source of reputation that they feel is most relevant. When multiple reputation collide, the one that is most related to the transaction at hand, and is most relevant to the person making the decision, should matter most. If a consumer, for example, cares more about a product's reliability, then they are likely to rely more on the reputation of the product than the store's. If what they care about is after-sale service, however, then the store's reputation is likely to be more influential. We should ask the same question as consumers and investors, not just on behalf of our companies: are we paying attention to the right sources of reputation when making decisions?
